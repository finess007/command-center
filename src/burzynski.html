What Are Antineoplastons?

Peptides and amino acid derivatives naturally occurring in human blood and urine. Discovered by Dr. Stanisław Burzynski during his PhD research at Lublin Medical University, Poland.

Key finding: Cancer patients have significantly lower levels of these peptides compared to healthy individuals. Reintroducing them appears to activate tumor-suppressing genes (p53, p21) and deactivate oncogenes.

How They Work

  • Molecular switches — regulate gene expression through demethylation and histone acetylation
  • Target 400+ mutated genes simultaneously (vs. commercial drugs targeting 1-3 genes each)
  • Non-toxic — confirmed by FDA's own safety reviews
  • Two main compounds: Antineoplaston A10 (phenylacetylglutamine) and AS2-1 (phenylbutyrate + phenylacetylglutamine)

Two Treatment Tracks

Track 1: Antineoplastons (10% of patients)

  • Original invention — peptide infusions via catheter, 24/7
  • FDA restricts access: must fail chemo/radiation first, then FDA reviews case individually
  • Available under Texas Right to Try Law for advanced patients
  • Targets 400+ genes simultaneously, essentially non-toxic

Track 2: Personalized Gene-Targeted Therapy (90% of patients)

  • Workaround because FDA blocks antineoplastons for most patients
  • Full genomic profiling (24,000 genes) via Caris Life Sciences
  • Cocktail of 5-7 commercially available FDA-approved drugs, prescribed off-label
  • PB (phenylbutyrate) tablets as the ANP component — can be prescribed off-label
  • Pioneered by Burzynski ~2000. MD Anderson announced the same approach in 2011 with $50M grant, calling it "revolutionary"

🧬 The Core Chemistry

Antineoplaston AS2-1 (the main compound)

  • Phenylbutyrate (PB) — sodium 4-phenylbutyrate, ATC code A16AX03
  • Phenylacetylglutamine (PAG) — the active metabolite
  • PB metabolizes into phenylacetate in the body → the primary active agent
  • PB is FDA-approved for urea cycle disorders → can be prescribed off-label for cancer

Mechanism of Action

  • Histone deacetylase inhibitor — more effective than structural analogs (2- and 3-phenylbutyrate)
  • Gene regulation: Activates p53 and p21 tumor suppressors via promoter demethylation
  • Amino acid uptake: Inhibits cancer cell absorption of L-glutamine and L-leucine
  • Induces differentiation: Forces cancer cells to mature/normalize instead of proliferating
  • Cytostasis: Halts cancer cell division

The Burzynski case reveals a repeating pattern of institutional capture that applies to any treatment threatening pharma revenue.
